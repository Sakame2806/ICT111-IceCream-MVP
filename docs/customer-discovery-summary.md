# Customer Discovery Summary

## Project Title
Pixic: Campus Digital Art Community Platform

## Target Users Studied
Digital Visual Artists (Student Illustrators, Designers) and Art Hobbyists on campus.

## Number of Responses / Interviews
10 Interviews (5 student artists, 3 hobbyists, and 2 art club leaders)

## Main Repeated Problem
Student artists lack a dedicated, organized campus platform to showcase their work, tag/categorize art by style/medium, and receive constructive peer feedback without their work getting buried in casual chat groups.

## Main Current Workarounds
- Ad-hoc Telegram/Line groups: Fast messaging but unstructured feeds where art posts are instantly buried.
- Personal Instagram/X accounts: Wide reach but heavily algorithm-dependent, offering little campus connection and mostly superficial engagement (likes/emojis).
- Google Drive folder links: Organizes portfolios but has zero social features, leading to no feedback or community discussions.

## Strongest Evidence Found
- 6 out of 10 interviewees noted that their art posts get buried within minutes in active group chats, leading to zero feedback.
- 4 artists admitted they stopped sharing art locally because there was no appreciation loop or organized way to archive and search portfolios.
- 8 out of 10 interviewees agreed that having art categories/tags (e.g., character design, 3D model, concept art) would save time and improve discovery.

## Weak Evidence or Unclear Findings
It is unclear if student artists would want web-only access or if they strictly require a mobile app, though a web platform is preferred for initial portfolio setup and high-resolution uploads.

## What We Must Revise
We originally assumed artists wanted a freelance commission matching platform immediately, but customer discovery revealed that they prioritize building a local community, showcase gallery, and feedback loop first.
